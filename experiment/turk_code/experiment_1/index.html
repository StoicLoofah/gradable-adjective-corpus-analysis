In this experiment, you will see some phrases displayed on the screen and you will be asked a question about each phrase. The experiment should take about 10 minutes.

Please do this experiment only if you are a native speaker of English, i.e. if English was the language spoken in your home when you are a child. If you are not a native English speaker, your results will be invalid.

You must accept the HIT in order to continue.

If you have questions about this research, please contact Dan Lassiter at danlassiter@stanford.edu or Noah Goodman at ngoodman@stanford.edu. You must be at least 18 years old to participate. Your participation in this research is voluntary. You may decline to answer any or all of the following questions. You may decline further participation, at any time, without adverse consequences. We're interested in how natural certain English phrases and sentences sound to you. For example, many English speakers feel that the sentence

Sam's speech infuriated completely Mary

sounds somewhat strange, even though the similar sentences

Sam's speech infuriated Mary completely

and

Sam's speech infuriated completely his audience on Saturday night

sound much more natural. We're interested in these kinds of feelings -- the feeling that a phrase or sentence is natural, in the sense that you would not find it surprising to hear a native English speaker say it in conversation. We will show you a number of phrases and ask you to make a judgment about how natural they sound.

We are not concerned with whether the sentence would be graded highly by a writing teacher, whether it conforms with grammar rules that you might have learned in school, etc. We're only concerned with whether the sentences could be said by a native speaker of English in normal daily speech.

For each phrase, we will ask you to select a radio button on a scale from 1 to 9, where 1 means that the sentence is completely unnatural and 9 means that it is completely natural. Don't think too hard about your answer -- there are no right or wrong answers, and this is not a memory or intelligence test. We are just interested in the first opinion you form about the sentences.

We're interested in whether you think certain phrases and sentences are meaningful English, i.e. whether an English speaker could reasonably use this sentence to communicate an idea. For example, many speakers would judge that the sentence

Sam's speech infuriated Mary happily

could not be used by an English speaker to communicate a coherent idea. In contrast, it's easy to imagine the similar sentence

Sam's speech infuriated Mary completely

being used. We're interested in your intuitions about this issue: whether you think that a phrase or sentence could be used meaningfully by an English speaker. We will show you a number of sentences and ask you to make a judgment about this.

We are not concerned with whether the phrase would be graded highly by a writing teacher, whether it conforms with grammar rules that you might have learned in school, etc. We're only concerned with whether the sentences could be said by a native speaker of English in normal daily speech.

For each phrase, we will ask you to select a radio button on a scale from 1 to 9, where 1 means that an English speaker definitely could not use this sentence, and 9 means that an English speaker definitely could use this sentence. Don't think too hard about your answer -- there are no right or wrong answers, and this is not a memory or intelligence test. We are just interested in the first opinion you form about the sentences.
